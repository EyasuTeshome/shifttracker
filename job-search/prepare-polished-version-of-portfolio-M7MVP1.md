## Refresher on what makes a good portfolio

Your portfolio is a tool that will help you to convince employers that you have the skills it takes to do the job because it provides evidence of what you are capable of. As you have learned, portfolios come in all sorts of shapes and colors, but solid and compelling ones share four attributes:

They are **simple**: there’s no extraneous information, only basic background information, contact information, and a project showcase.
They are **visual**: they use screenshots, mockups, live demos, and other visual tools to show the viewer what you’re capable of making.
They are **thorough**: while the design itself may be simple, they make use of links (both in-text and otherwise) to show extra background information about their work, projects, and history.
And they **look good**: they use 1-3 elegant fonts, focus on 2-3 different colors, use HD images, and use color palettes that look good together.

## **Exercise**

### **Instructions**

For software developers, building a strong portfolio will require several rounds. This first refinement aims to make sure that you share with your career coach the best possible version of your portfolio. Your coach will identify the specific aspects that you need to improve and both of you will work on them during the week.

As in the case of other application materials, an effective strategy is to go through your portfolio several times, focusing each time on revising a particular attribute, such as content, design, and functionality. The following steps will help you verify that your portfolio looks professional and reflects your latest skills and projects: 

1. Read through your portfolio one first time and analyze the content: **what it says and shows about you**. Ask yourself if it provides a compelling picture of your knowledge, skills, and experience as a software developer. 
Do the projects that you selected give a broad picture of your skills?
Does it showcase your most important skills and all the tools you master? 
Is it enough, but not too much?
Make a note of information that might be missing or anything that is not essential. Identify any necessary edits and make the changes. As you go through the portfolio, make note of any link that is not working or any error you identify, but don’t focus on testing functionality or fixing mistakes. 

2. Continue now by looking carefully at the **evidence that you are providing of your knowledge and skills**. Employers will only know that you’re able to write good code if they can view or test that code themselves. Make sure that each one of your portfolio projects has links to its respective GitHub repository and that you include live demo links wherever possible so that everyone—whether they understand software development or not—will be able to look at your projects and gain an understanding of your capabilities.
 
3. Go through your portfolio once more, this time looking at the design, that is focus on the **format: how it looks.** Start first by using a desktop computer or laptop. Open each screen and analyze it carefully. Use these questions to guide your revision:
     - Did you follow the general structure of the template you chose? It’s ok if you personalized it to reflect your strengths, but make sure that you followed the general structure. 
     - Is the design simple, but attractive?
     - What about the colors? 
     - And the fonts? Avoid using more than 3 different fonts.
     - Are the styles applied consistently? 
     - Are all the texts readable? Verify that you are using sans-serif fonts and use italics with caution because they are harder to read online.
     - How about the images and screenshots? Are they good quality and professional-looking?
Make any necessary changes in the format now.

4. To verify that your portfolio is **responsive**, now look at all the different sections on a mobile device or use an emulator like mobiReady to check how it looks on screens of different sizes. 

5. Go through your portfolio again, this time focusing on **functionality**. Open each screen once again and analyze if all the elements included work properly:
     - The page should load in no more than 3 seconds.
     - Check if images and screenshots load fast enough, and if they don’t, decrease the image file size.
     - Verify that all links take you to the correct pages. 

6. Now, check these other important details in your portfolio:
     - Have you included **attributions** for every photo, icon, and web concept you used? They can be integrated in the footer of the webpage or in a separate page. You can mention that you implemented a portfolio concept designed by {designer_name}; you can also give credit to the artists whose photos you used.
     - Do you have a **contact page, contact form or email** visible? You want employers to get in touch with you!
     - Have you added **Google Analytics** to your portfolio? Analytics will let you gather information about the number of visit you receive in each section. This information will help you identify which of your application materials are strongest or weakest, and which need to be improved.
     - (OPTIONAL) Are you using a **personalized domain name**? This can highly improve the credibility of your portfolio.
   
7. It’s time to revise the **text** that you included in your portfolio! Read each phrase carefully. Make sure that sentences state complete ideas and that each idea is clearly stated. Identify any grammar or spelling errors, and correct them.

8. As a final step, analyze if you meet each and every one of the requirements of the [portfolio rubric](https://docs.google.com/document/d/11zQJ2GF9AJoh79Ije18xeujE08u1AEpsCrX-Lus9SPo/edit?usp=sharing).  Use the [Assess your portfolio rubric worksheet](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1BPBwnvmj5ZnPvPlUAHt_XN90M9Cvni48-okz60OwDp0/edit?usp=sharing) to register your scores and find the learning resources that you can use to improve your portfolio. **You don’t need to submit this worksheet**. Keep it at hand as a reference during this week’s round of revisions.

Probably, you are thinking that these are too many steps. Well, you will be surprised at how much your portfolio will impact your job opportunities. **Your portfolio demonstrates to employers the quality of your products and shows them that you can take care of details, so it’s worth it to make sure there are no errors**.
