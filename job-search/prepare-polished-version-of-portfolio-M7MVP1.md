## Refresher on what makes a good portfolio

Your portfolio is a tool that will help you convince employers that you have the skills it takes to do the job because it provides evidence of what you are capable of. As you have learned, portfolios come in all sorts of shapes and colors, but solid and compelling ones share four attributes:

- They are **simple**: there’s no extraneous information, only basic background information, contact information, and a project showcase.
- They are **visual**: they use screenshots, mockups, live demos, and other visual tools to show the viewer what you’re capable of making.
- They are **thorough**: while the design itself may be simple, they make use of links (both in-text and otherwise) to show extra background information about the work, projects, and history.
- And they **look good**: they use 1-3 elegant fonts, focus on 2-4 different colors, use HD images, and use color palettes that are attractive and harmonious.

## **Exercise**

### **Instructions**

For software developers, building a strong portfolio will require several rounds. This first exercise aims to make sure that you share with your career coach the best possible version of your portfolio. Your coach will identify the specific aspects that you need to improve and both of you will work on them during the week.

As in the case of other application materials, an effective strategy is to go through your portfolio several times, focusing each one on revising a particular attribute, such as content, design, or functionality. 

1. Read through your portfolio one first time and analyze the content: **what it says and shows about you**. Ask yourself if it provides a compelling picture of your knowledge, skills, and experience as a software developer. 
     - Do the projects that you selected give a broad picture of your skills?
     - Does it showcase your most important skills and all the tools you master? 
     - Is it enough, but not too much?
Identify information that might be missing or anything that is not essential and make the changes. As you go through the portfolio, make note of any link that is not working or any error you identify, but don’t focus on testing functionality or fixing mistakes. 

2. Continue now by looking carefully at the **evidence that you are providing of your knowledge and skills: links to the respective Github repository and to live demos, when possible**. Employers will only know that you’re able to write good code if they can view or test that code themselves or, if they can look at your projects.
 
3. Go through your portfolio once more, this time looking at the **design or how it looks.** Open each screen in a computer or laptop and analyze it carefully:
     - Did you follow the general structure of the template you chose? It’s ok if you personalized it to reflect your strengths, but make sure that you followed the general structure. 
     - Is the design simple, but attractive?
     - Are the styles applied consistently? 
     - Did you use 2-4 attractive colors?
     - Did you use 1-3 sans-serif fonts?
     - Are all the texts readable?
     - How about the images and screenshots? Are they good quality and professional-looking?
Make any necessary changes in the format now.

4. To verify that your portfolio is **responsive**, now open it on a mobile device or use an emulator like mobiReady to check how it looks on screens of different sizes. 

5. Go through your portfolio again, this time focusing on **functionality**. Open each screen once again and verify that all the elements included work properly:
     - Each page should load in no more than 3 seconds.
     - Check if images and screenshots load fast enough; if they don’t, decrease the image file size.
     - Verify that all links take you to the correct pages. 

6. Check other important details in your portfolio:
     - Have you included **attributions** for every photo, icon, and web concept you used? They can be integrated in the footer of the webpage or in a separate page. 
     - Do you have a **contact page, contact form or email** visible? You want employers to get in touch with you!
     - (OPTIONAL) Are you using a **personalized domain name**? This can highly improve the credibility of your portfolio.
   
7. Now, revise the **text** that you included in your portfolio! Read each phrase carefully. Make sure that sentences state complete ideas and that each idea is clearly stated. Identify any grammar or spelling errors, and correct them.

8. Finally, analyze if you meet each and every one of the requirements of the [portfolio rubric](https://docs.google.com/document/d/11zQJ2GF9AJoh79Ije18xeujE08u1AEpsCrX-Lus9SPo/edit?usp=sharing).  Use the [Assess your portfolio rubric worksheet](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1BPBwnvmj5ZnPvPlUAHt_XN90M9Cvni48-okz60OwDp0/edit?usp=sharing) to register your scores and find the learning resources that you can use to improve your portfolio. **You don’t need to submit this worksheet**. Keep it at hand as a reference during this week’s round of revisions.

Probably, you are thinking that these are too many steps. Well, you will be surprised at how much your portfolio will impact your job opportunities! **Your portfolio demonstrates to employers the quality of your products and shows them that you can take care of details, so it’s worth it to make sure there are no errors**.
